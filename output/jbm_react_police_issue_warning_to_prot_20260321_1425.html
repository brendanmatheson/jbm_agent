UK political stability has halved since 2000, sitting at just 0.51 today versus a peak of 1.03

Police warnings before London protests reflect a country whose political stability is still only half what it was at the millennium — despite having among the strongest rule-of-law institutions in the developed world

The framing around police warnings at al-Quds Day rallies tends to focus on the specific event — but the data reveals a deeper, longer story. UK political stability peaked at 1.03 in 2000, crashed to a historic low of just 0.08 in 2018 — the year of Brexit paralysis and the Salisbury poisoning — and has only partially recovered to 0.51 by 2023. That recovery, while real, still leaves the UK at roughly half its millennial-era stability level. Yet simultaneously, the UK ranks 3rd out of 8 peer nations on rule of law, scoring 1.40 — ahead of France, Spain, Italy and the United States.

Key findings
Long-run

UK political stability hit a trough of just 0.08 in 2018 — a near-total collapse from its peak of 1.03 in 2000 — and even after five years of recovery sits at only 0.51 in 2023, meaning the country remains in a structurally more volatile state than at any point in the early 2000s

International

Despite its reputation for robust policing of protests, the UK's rule of law score of 1.40 in 2023 places it 3rd among 8 peer nations, behind only the Netherlands (1.64) and Germany (1.55), and comfortably ahead of France (1.18), Spain (0.82) and Italy (0.39)

International

France — which has seen far more severe crackdowns on protests in recent years, from the gilets jaunes to pension reform demonstrations — scores only 0.34 on political stability in 2023, more than a third lower than the UK's 0.51, suggesting the UK's protest environment is comparatively contained

Weekly news coverage of protest policing in the UK focuses almost entirely on individual events and police tactics — but the data tells a more uncomfortable structural story. The UK's rule-of-law institutions are genuinely among the strongest in the democratic world, yet political stability has only recovered to half its year-2000 level after a historic nadir in 2018. The real question the coverage is missing isn't whether police should warn protesters — it's why, after a quarter century, British political stability has settled into a new, permanently lower equilibrium.
